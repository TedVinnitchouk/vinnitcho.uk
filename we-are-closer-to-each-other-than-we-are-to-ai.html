We are closer to each other than we are to AI
April 6, 2026
Robert Putnam’s work on social capital traces a long arc in American life between individualism and collective identity...
The evolution of the internet can be understood as a compressed version of this same cycle...
The rise of consumer social media marked a meaningful shift toward “We.”...
That structure began to erode as platforms optimized for engagement...
This marked a return to an “I” phase, but one that differed from the early internet...
From Putnam’s perspective, this shift has significant implications...
It is in this context that shared cultural events take on renewed importance...
Sports remain one of the clearest examples of this dynamic...
Films and music, when they reach broad audiences, perform a similar function...
Live events and physical gatherings amplify these effects...
As AI continues to improve, the forces driving individualization are likely to intensify...
This suggests that the current trajectory is not simply one of increasing isolation...
Putnam’s framework implies that periods of heightened individualism generate their own response...
The movement from “I” back toward “We” will not be driven by the systems that fragment experience, but by the human need to share it.
