We are closer to each other than we are to AI
April 6, 2026
Robert Putnam’s work on social capital traces a long arc in American life between individualism and collective identity. In Bowling Alone and later in The Upswing, he documents how the United States moved from a deeply communal society into a period of rising individualism, marked by declining participation in civic institutions and weaker community ties. This trajectory is often described as an “I-We-I” curve.
The evolution of the internet can be understood as a compressed version of this same cycle. In its earliest form, the internet was fundamentally individual. Users searched, read, and consumed. Publishing was limited. This was an “I” phase defined by access rather than participation.
The rise of consumer social media marked a shift toward “We.” Platforms recreated real world relationships online. People shared content with friends and engaged with people they already knew. The internet strengthened existing networks.
That structure eroded as platforms optimized for engagement. Discovery replaced relationships. Algorithms replaced the social graph. Users shifted from producing for their network to consuming from a global pool.
This marked a return to an “I” phase, but one that differed from the early internet. Each user now lives in a personalized stream. Two people in the same room can consume entirely different realities.
Putnam’s insight becomes critical here. Social capital depends on shared experiences. Without overlap, connection becomes harder. Conversation requires more effort. Community weakens not because people do not want it, but because the conditions that enable it disappear.
This is why shared cultural events matter. Sports, films, music, and live events align attention and create common ground. A live game cannot be personalized. Millions watch the same moment unfold in real time.
As AI improves, personalization will intensify. Experiences will diverge further. Shared experiences will become more scarce and therefore more valuable.
We are not moving toward isolation. We are moving toward contrast. Highly individualized digital lives paired with increasing demand for shared real world experiences.
The more the internet fragments us, the more we seek each other.
