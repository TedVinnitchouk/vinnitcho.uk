We are closer to each other than we are to AI
April 6, 2026
Robert Putnam’s work on social capital traces a long arc in American life between individualism and collective identity. In Bowling Alone and later in The Upswing, he documents how the United States moved from a deeply communal society in the early twentieth century into a period of rising individualism by the late twentieth century, marked by declining participation in civic institutions, lower interpersonal trust, and weaker community ties. This trajectory is often described as an “I–We–I” curve: a movement away from collective life toward individual autonomy, followed by the conditions that eventually make a return to shared identity both necessary and inevitable. Putnam’s central claim is not simply that community has declined, but that social cohesion depends on repeated, overlapping experiences that bind individuals into a common fabric. When those experiences disappear, the fabric weakens.
The evolution of the internet can be understood as a compressed version of this same cycle. In its earliest form, the internet was fundamentally individual. It provided unprecedented access to information, but the experience was largely solitary. Users searched, read, and consumed. Publishing existed, but it was limited to those with technical expertise or institutional backing. The average user engaged with the internet as a personal tool rather than a shared space. This was an “I” phase defined by access and exploration, not participation or community.
The rise of consumer social media marked a meaningful shift toward “We.” Platforms such as Facebook recreated existing social structures online by organizing interaction around real world relationships. People shared content with their friends, followed individuals they knew, and participated in conversations grounded in shared context. The internet, in this period, reinforced offline networks rather than replacing them. It created a layer of connectivity that strengthened social capital by increasing the frequency and visibility of interaction within existing communities. Cultural consumption, while still digital, retained a degree of overlap because it was filtered through the social graph. People encountered content because someone they knew shared it, not because an algorithm selected it.
This structure began to erode as platforms optimized for engagement and scale. The shift from a “who you know” model to a discovery driven model fundamentally changed the nature of social media. Platforms such as TikTok introduced feeds that prioritized content based on predicted interest rather than social connection. In this system, virality replaced familiarity as the organizing principle. Users were no longer primarily producers of content for their immediate networks. They became consumers of content produced by a global pool of creators, filtered and ranked by algorithms that increasingly individualized each user’s experience.
This marked a return to an “I” phase, but one that differed from the early internet. The initial “I” phase was defined by limited participation and broad exposure to shared information sources. The current phase is defined by active participation within highly personalized environments. Each user inhabits a distinct cultural stream, shaped by behavioral data and optimized for engagement. The overlap that once existed as a byproduct of shared platforms and social networks is no longer guaranteed. Two individuals in the same physical space can consume entirely different sets of media, with little common ground.
From Putnam’s perspective, this shift has significant implications. Social capital is built on shared experiences that create common reference points and facilitate interaction. When individuals no longer encounter the same inputs, the ease of connection diminishes. Conversation requires more effort, as fewer assumptions can be made about what others have seen or experienced. Community becomes harder to sustain, not because individuals no longer seek it, but because the underlying conditions that enable it have changed.
It is in this context that shared cultural events take on renewed importance. Historically, mass media functioned as a coordinating mechanism. Television broadcasts, major films, and widely followed sporting events aligned attention across large populations. They created moments where individuals, regardless of background, engaged with the same content at the same time. This simultaneity mattered. It allowed for immediate conversation, collective interpretation, and the formation of shared narratives that extended beyond the event itself.
Sports remain one of the clearest examples of this dynamic. A live game unfolds in real time, with an outcome that is uncertain and cannot be personalized. The experience is inherently collective. Millions of viewers watch, react, and process the same sequence of events simultaneously. This creates a shared temporal and emotional framework that facilitates connection. The significance of the event lies not only in the game itself, but in the fact that it is experienced together.
Films and music, when they reach broad audiences, perform a similar function. A widely viewed film or a culturally dominant album establishes a common set of references and meanings. These works provide a shared language that individuals can draw upon in conversation and social interaction. They reduce the friction of connection by supplying a baseline of mutual understanding. Even in an increasingly fragmented media landscape, certain cultural products still achieve this level of penetration, and when they do, their impact extends beyond entertainment.
Live events and physical gatherings amplify these effects by combining shared content with shared space. They reintroduce proximity as a factor in experience. Individuals are not only consuming the same material, but doing so in the presence of others. This reinforces social bonds through direct interaction and visible collective response. In Putnam’s terms, these are the kinds of repeated, overlapping experiences that rebuild social capital.
As AI continues to improve, the forces driving individualization are likely to intensify. Personalization will become more precise, and the divergence between individual experiences will increase. In such an environment, shared experiences become structurally scarce. Their value does not diminish as a result. It increases. They serve as anchors that counterbalance fragmentation by reintroducing common ground.
This suggests that the current trajectory is not simply one of increasing isolation, but of increasing contrast between individualized digital life and collective physical experience. The more the former expands, the more necessary the latter becomes. Shared identities, cultural moments, and communal events provide the overlap required for meaningful connection in a world where overlap is no longer the default.
Putnam’s framework implies that periods of heightened individualism generate their own response. As social capital declines, individuals seek new forms of connection that restore a sense of belonging. In the context of the internet and AI, this response is likely to manifest in a renewed emphasis on experiences that cannot be personalized. Sports, films, music, and community gatherings are not remnants of an earlier era. They are the mechanisms through which collective life is reconstituted.
The movement from “I” back toward “We” will not be driven by the systems that fragment experience, but by the human need to share it.
